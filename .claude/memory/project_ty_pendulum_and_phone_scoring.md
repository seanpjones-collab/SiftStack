## 1. Pendulum Theory

**Core principle:** Marketing channels follow a predictable cycle — discovery, adoption, saturation, decline. The "right channel today is the wrong channel in six months." The pendulum represents what is performing well right now and the window of opportunity in marketing — that window is always closing. We have to defend against what is performing well now, because it may not be performing well in two years, a year, or six months.

**Saturation defined:** When too many investors pile into the same marketing channel. Costs rise, response rates drop. It is structural (too many competitors), not cyclical/seasonal. Texting went from "send 2K texts and get a deal" in 2018 to nearly unusable by 2020.

**Channel history (Ty's verbatim timeline):**
- **2018 — Texting wide open.** Send 2,000–3,000 texts, get a deal back. Almost nobody was doing it. Then mass-blasting tools emerged, compliance tightened, carriers started filtering investor texts.
- **2021 — Direct Mail became the move.** Sellers' mailboxes were empty (their phones were ringing off the hook). 3,000 mailers = 1 deal regardless of list. Then investors flooded mailboxes — homeowners got 10+ "We Buy Houses" postcards a month.
- **2022 — PPC wide open.** Google Ads for "sell my house fast" had almost no competition. Cost per lead was low, quality was high. Then everyone jumped on, CPL tripled in competitive markets.
- **2023 — Pay-Per-Lead (PPL) took over.** Buy the lead instead of running ads. Lead quality dropped — same leads sold to 5+ investors. Sellers had already talked to three people that morning.
- **2024 — Cold Calling came back.** All inbound channels saturated. The pendulum swung back to outbound. Phones went quiet, sellers started answering again.

**Why operators get wiped out (the Trap):** Honeymoon → Crash. Channel works for 2–4 months ("This is where people think they've made it"), deals flow, operator goes all-in. By month 6+, performance drops off a cliff. They spent $5–10K on a mailer and it stopped working — the channel didn't break, it got crowded.

**Defensive diversification rules (Do This / Not This):**
- **Do:** Run 3+ channels simultaneously; start with cheapest and add expensive later; track cost per deal by channel; expect every channel to eventually saturate.
- **Don't:** Go all-in on whatever is "hot" right now; start with the most expensive channel; assume your best channel will work forever; chase the next trend when one channel dies.
- **Stack, don't replace.** "You don't graduate from cheap channels. You stack on top of them. A text costs a penny. Keep texting. Add calling. Then add mail. The mistake is replacing cheap with expensive instead of layering."
- **PPC = safety net, not primary.** If running outbound, also be "seen" (PPC, SEO, Facebook) so missed outbound leads have a path back. Inbound is the backup plan, not the main play. Inbound-heavy strategies require continuous high spend; when the market gets competitive, costs accelerate (don't plateau).
- **Start with two channels, not five.** "You need two. A text plus a call. That's your starting point. Add the third channel only after the first two are producing consistently."

**Single-Channel vs Multi-Channel scorecard:**
- Single Channel: Deal Consistency 2/10, Recovery Speed = months, Monthly Variance 60%, Dependency Risk = Critical.
- Multi-Channel wins on every dimension.

**Pendulum-Proof self-assessment (5 dimensions, 1–5 each, 25 total):** Channel Diversity, Cost Awareness, Saturation Monitoring, Sequential System, Recovery Plan.

**Cautionary tale — Nate Beekman:** Scaled PPC to $40K/mo on "sell my house fast" Google Ads. Worked, then stopped. CPL tripled, lead quality dropped, burnout. Fix wasn't more spend — it was rebuilding lean with foundational outbound channels. PPC at $40K/mo → Market saturated → CPL tripled → Burnout → Rebuilt lean.

**Saturation Signals (leading indicators):** Declining response rates, rising cost per lead, more competitors using the same channel in your market.

## 2. Channel Cost Economics (Cheapest First, Always)

| Order | Channel | Cost per Touch |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | SMS / Text | **$0.01** |
| 2 | Cold Calling | **$0.03–0.06** |
| 3 | Direct Mail | **$0.50–0.75** |
| 4 | Deep Prospecting | **$1.50–4.00** |
| 5 | Door Knocking | **$25–50** |

**The 2,500x gap.** SMS to door-knock spans 2,500x. "That's not a rounding error. That's the difference between running marketing for six months and running out of money in six weeks."

**Three sequencing tiers:**
1. **Cheapest First (foundation):** Bulk SMS + cold calling. Pennies per touch. Always your foundation.
2. **Mid-Tier:** Direct mail + deep prospecting. Higher cost per touch, broader reach for leads that didn't convert on outbound.
3. **Premium:** Door knocking + deep research. Most expensive per touch but highest conversion for hardest-to-reach leads.

## 3. Channel Transitions

- **Move from N → N+1 only after stacking, not replacing.** Keep cheap channel running; add the next layer on top.
- **Add the third channel only after the first two are producing consistently.** Trying to launch everything at once is the fastest path to doing nothing well.
- **Pipeline order (verbatim from phone-scoring page):** Data Pull → Skip Trace → Phone Score → Tag & Filter → Load Dialer → Call.
- **In sequential marketing:** SMS → Call → Mail → Deep Prospecting (Pendulum-defense ordering).
- **Watch saturation signals** to know when a channel is dying: declining response rate, rising CPL, more local competitors using the same channel.

## 4. Phone Scoring 5-Tier Framework

Trestle scores every phone number 0–100 based on real activity data. Five tiers map score to dial action.

| Tier | Score | % of records | Correct Rate | Dead Rate | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Dial First** | **81–100** | 43.4% (881 phones) | 30.3% | 1.9% | Call AND text immediately. Load first into dialer. Day 1 priority in niche sequential; fills ReadyMode queue first in bulk. |
| **Dial Second** | **61–80** | 4.9% (99 phones) | 11.1% | 11.1% | Call after Dial First exhausted. Strong activity, active lines. |
| **Dial Third** | **41–60** | 5.0% (102 phones) | 8.8% | 12.7% | Call if capacity allows. Moderate activity. Dial 1–3 is the safe conservative approach (correct numbers still appear here). |
| **Dial Fourth** | **21–40** | 33.1% (671 phones) | 1.0% | 34.6% | Low priority. Route to direct mail only. 99/100 dials produce nothing. |
| **Drop** | **0–20** | 13.6% (275 phones) | 0.7% | 33.0% | DO NOT DIAL. Remove from call lists entirely. Send direct mail to property address instead. Every dial here burns caller ID for a 0.7% chance of a contact. |

**The combined-tier insight:** Dial First + Dial Second = 980 phones (48.3%) containing **92% of all Correct numbers**. Dial Fourth + Drop = 946 phones (46.7%) holding only 8% of Correct but **82% of all Dead numbers**. Tagging cuts your call list nearly in half while keeping virtually all good numbers.

**Bimodal distribution:** Scores cluster at 30 (662 phones) and 100 (819 phones). Very little middle ground. The API essentially says "probably dead" or "probably active" — no ambiguity.

## 6. The 50% Dead-Number Rule

- **~50% of any blind list is dead.** Disconnected, fax machines, or inactive lines.
- **Industry blind-dial connect rate: 2–3%.** Callers connect with 4–6 people per 200 dials.
- **Post-scoring connect rate: 9.5%** (Joshua English split-test with a VA) — **4.75x improvement** from one change.
- **The waste math:** 150 dials/day minimum per caller. If 50% are dead, that's 75 wasted dials at $0.03–0.06 each = **$2.25–$4.50/day per caller** in pure waste = **$50–100/month per caller** in burned resources, plus degraded caller IDs that take weeks to recover.
- **Three things happen behind the scenes when you blind-dial:**
  1. Every dead number trains carrier algorithms to flag your number as spam.
  2. Callers lose momentum (nobody stays sharp after 75 dead dials in a row).
  3. Cost per contact quietly doubles because half of dialer time produces zero conversations.
- **"Your caller ID is a depreciating asset. Every dead number you dial is a withdrawal from that account. Score first, dial second."**

## 7. Pre-Dial Workflow

**Order of operations:** Data Pull → **Skip Trace → Phone Score → Tag & Filter → Load Dialer → Call.**

Phone scoring is the **quality gate between skip tracing and dialing.**

**Six setup steps (30 minutes from zero to scored & loaded):**
1. **Create a Trestle account** at trestleiq.com. Requires a **work email** (Gmail/Yahoo/free providers rejected). ~$0.015 per phone scored.
2. **Export phone numbers from DataSift** in Trestle's required column format.
3. **Run the Claude Phone Validator skill** — automates Trestle API submission, score retrieval, and 5-tier tag generation.
4. **Download scored results** — every number with 0–100 activity score and Dial First/Second/Third/Fourth/Drop tag.
5. **Apply tags inside DataSift** — upload scored results back, tag by phone number (CSV bulk upload, individual record, or import-time mapping).
6. **Load prioritized records into dialer** — filter by tag. Dial First into smrtPhone or ReadyMode → Dial Second → route Dial Fourth + Drop to direct mail only.

**Pipeline by campaign size:**
- **Niche Sequential (200–500 records):** Score all numbers. Call Dial First → Dial Third over 3 days using click-to-dial (smrtPhone). Route Dial Fourth to direct mail only. Drop is removed entirely.
- **Bulk Sequential (5,000+ records):** Score all numbers. Load only Dial First + Dial Second into ReadyMode power dialer. Remaining tiers route to SMS-only or direct mail. Never waste dialer lines on low-score numbers.

**Two-layer caller-ID protection:**
- Layer 1 — Phone scoring removes dead numbers from your list.
- Layer 2 — **Free Caller Registry** (freecallerregistry.com/fcr) removes your numbers from spam databases. Free, takes 5 minutes, **re-register every 3 months** (registrations expire). Won't fix already-flagged numbers but prevents clean numbers from getting flagged.

**Tags applied:**
- **Trestle priority tags (5):** Dial First, Dial Second, Dial Third, Dial Fourth, Drop.
- **Relationship tags (skip-trace context):** Daughter, Husband, Son, Wife, Grandchild, Relative, plus Spanish Speaking for language routing.
